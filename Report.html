NOC Guidelines

Guidance on how to obtain NOC

To obtain a No Objection Certificate (NOC) for groundwater extraction in India, follow these general guidelines based on the context provided:

1. Eligibility Check:
- Determine if your industry, infrastructure project, or mining project falls under the categories that require an NOC for groundwater extraction.

2. Online Application:
- The entire process of obtaining an NOC is online through a web-based application system. Visit the Central Ground Water Authority (CGWA) or the concerned State/UT Ground Water Authority's website to access the application portal.

3. Documentation:
- Prepare the necessary documents required for the application process, which may include:
- Details of water requirement as per the National Building Code.
- Affidavit confirming non/ inadequate availability of public water supply.
- Certificate of non-availability of water from local government water supply agency.
- Water quality data if extracting saline groundwater.
- Rainwater harvesting plan.
- Impact assessment report for certain projects.

4. Submission:
- Submit the completed application along with the required documents through the online portal.

5. Compliance:
- Ensure compliance with the specific conditions for obtaining the NOC, such as installation of water flow meters, rainwater harvesting systems, and sewage treatment plants as applicable.

6. Monitoring and Renewal:
- After obtaining the NOC, monitor and comply with the conditions mentioned in the certificate.
- Renew the NOC periodically by applying for renewal at least ninety days before the expiry date.

7. Payment:
- Be prepared to pay ground water abstraction charges as per the rates mentioned in the guidelines.

8. Follow-up:
- Keep track of the status of your application and any communication from the authorities regarding the NOC.

9. Compliance Monitoring:
- Be aware that District Collectors/Deputy Commissioners (DCs) /District Magistrates (DMs) are authorized to take enforcement measures in case of non-compliance.

10. Extension or Modification:
- If needed, apply for an extension of the NOC with valid reasons and necessary documentation.

Remember, the specific requirements and procedures may vary based on the type of project and the state regulations. It is advisable to refer to the official guidelines and consult with the relevant authorities for detailed and up-to-date information on obtaining an NOC for groundwater extraction in India.

Conditions for obtaining NOC for ground water extraction

To obtain a No Objection Certificate (NOC) for groundwater extraction in India, the following conditions need to be met based on the provided context:

1. Application Process:
- All new/existing industries, infrastructure projects, and mining projects abstracting groundwater must apply for an NOC from the Central Ground Water Authority (CGWA) or the concerned State/UT Ground Water Authority.

2. Online Application:
- The entire process of obtaining the NOC is online through a web-based application system.

3. Exemptions:
- Certain categories of consumers like individual domestic consumers, rural drinking water supply schemes, armed forces establishments, agricultural activities, and micro/small enterprises drawing limited groundwater may be exempt from seeking an NOC.

4. Documentation:
- Applicants need to submit various documents with the application, including details of water requirement, affidavits confirming non-availability of public water supply, certificates of non-availability of water, water quality data for saline groundwater extraction, and rainwater harvesting plans.

5. Compliance:
- Proponents must comply with specific conditions to obtain the NOC, such as installing water flow meters, sewage treatment plants, and rainwater harvesting systems as required.

6. Monitoring and Reporting:
- Proponents are required to monitor and report on groundwater levels, water quality, and compliance with the conditions of the NOC periodically.

7. Penalties and Charges:
- Industries abstracting groundwater are liable to pay groundwater abstraction charges based on the quantum of groundwater proposed to be extracted as per the specified rates.

8. Renewal:
- NOCs need to be renewed periodically, subject to compliance with the conditions mentioned therein. Renewal applications should be submitted at least ninety days before the expiry date.

9. Extension:
- In case of genuine reasons for delay in well construction during the validity period of the NOC, applicants can apply for an extension, which is granted for a maximum period of two years.

10. Delegation of Powers:
- District Magistrates/Collectors have the authority to seal illegal wells, disconnect electricity supply, and take enforcement measures against offenders related to groundwater extraction.

These conditions outline the key steps and requirements for obtaining an NOC for groundwater extraction in India. It is essential to adhere to these guidelines and fulfill the necessary criteria to ensure compliance with regulations and sustainable management of groundwater resources.

Training Opportunities

Training opportunities related to groundwater in India are offered by various government agencies, research institutions, and universities. These programs are designed for professionals, students, and individuals interested in the management, conservation, and sustainable use of groundwater resources. Here are some key organizations and programs that offer training in this field:

1. Central Ground Water Board (CGWB):
- Offers regular training programs on groundwater management, hydrogeology, groundwater modeling, and resource assessment.
- Organizes workshops and seminars on topics like artificial recharge, rainwater harvesting, and groundwater quality management.

2. National Institute of Hydrology (NIH):
- Provides specialized short-term training courses on groundwater hydrology, modeling, remote sensing applications, and water resource management.
- Tailored training programs can be arranged for organizations and professionals.

3. Ground Water Department of Various States:
- Many state groundwater departments offer training programs focusing on state-specific issues, regulation, and management practices.
- Provides hands-on field training for groundwater surveying and monitoring.

4. Indian Institute of Technology (IITs):
- Offers professional development programs and short-term courses in water resources management, including groundwater.
- Provides research-based training for those interested in advanced studies in groundwater hydrology and related fields.

5. TERI (The Energy and Resources Institute):
- Conducts training workshops on groundwater governance, management, and sustainable water use practices.
- Offers capacity-building programs targeted at policy-makers, government officials, and NGOs in the water sector.

6. Centre for Science and Environment (CSE):
- Provides online and in-person training programs on water management, including courses on groundwater conservation and rainwater harvesting.
- Offers specialized courses focusing on urban water management, groundwater recharge, and usage.

7. International Water Management Institute (IWMI):
- Offers training programs on integrated water resource management, including groundwater management as a key component.
- Collaborates with Indian institutions to offer region-specific training programs.

8. National Water Academy (NWA):
- Provides training in various aspects of water management, including groundwater management, watershed development, and hydrological modeling.
- Programs are aimed at engineers, hydrogeologists, and water resource managers.

9. Indira Gandhi National Open University (IGNOU):
- Offers distance learning courses in water resource management, including modules on groundwater.
- Provides flexible learning options suitable for working professionals.

These training programs and courses offer valuable opportunities to enhance skills and knowledge in groundwater management, contributing significantly to better water resource management in India.

Ground Water Management Practices

Groundwater management practices involve various strategies and techniques to ensure sustainable use and conservation of groundwater resources. Some key practices include:

1. Water Audits:
- Conducting systematic water audits to measure the flow of water from withdrawal or treatment sites to its usage and discharge points.
- Calculating water balance, identifying water use patterns, and finding ways to save water.
- Detailed water audits involve on-site training, system analysis, monitoring, leak detection, water quality assessment, and developing water balance reports.

2. Water Conservation Measures:
- Setting up norms for water budgeting to track water usage.
- Modernizing industrial processes to reduce water consumption.
- Implementing water recycling systems like re-circulating cooling systems.
- Using innovative approaches like ozonation cooling water to reduce water wastage.
- Reusing wastewater for non-potable purposes like gardening.
- Ensuring proper processing of effluents to meet disposal norms.

3. Groundwater Recharge Enhancement:
- Encouraging practices like rainwater harvesting to replenish aquifers.
- Implementing aquifer recharge enhancement techniques to increase groundwater availability.
- Promoting urban wastewater reuse for non-drinking purposes.

4. Water Balance and Quality Mapping:
- Developing detailed water balance reports for facilities to understand water consumption and discharge patterns.
- Mapping water quality requirements at different user areas to identify opportunities for water recycling and reuse.

5. Water Saving Opportunities:
- Identifying specific areas where water savings can be achieved through efficiency improvements.
- Implementing strategies to reduce water wastage and optimize water use.
- Providing recommendations on how to save water, including investment requirements and implementation methods.

6. Training and Capacity Building:
- Providing training programs for industry personnel on water management best practices.
- Educating stakeholders on the importance of water conservation and sustainable groundwater use.
- Building capacity within industries to adopt water-saving technologies and practices.

By implementing these groundwater management practices, industries can optimize water use, reduce wastage, and contribute to sustainable water resource management.

Definition of GroundWater Terms

Here is the Glossary of technical terms used in the context of Groundwater Management Practices:

1. Safe area: An area categorized as safe from the groundwater resources point of view based on the latest groundwater resources assessment.

2. Semi-critical area: An area categorized as semi-critical from the groundwater resources point of view based on the latest groundwater resources assessment.

3. Critical area: An area categorized as critical from the groundwater resources point of view based on the latest groundwater resources assessment.

4. Over-exploited area: An area categorized as over-exploited from the groundwater resources point of view based on the latest groundwater resources assessment.

5. Aquifer: A geological formation capable of storing and transmitting groundwater.

6. Deeper Aquifer: In areas with multiple aquifer systems, the aquifer(s) occurring below the uppermost aquifer.

7. Well: Any structure used for the extraction of groundwater, including open wells, dug wells, bore wells, tube wells, filter points, collector wells, infiltration galleries, recharge wells, or any of their combinations or variations.

8. Government Agency: A Central or State Government body.

9. Supplier: A Government or Government-approved Water Supply Agency.

10. Mine: An area where mining activity is taking place or an area abandoned after mining.

11. Illegal Groundwater Abstraction Structure: An energized abstraction structure like a dug well, tube well, or bore well used to withdraw groundwater without a valid No Objection Certificate.

12. Rainwater Harvesting: The technique or system of collecting and storing rainwater for future use or groundwater recharge.

13. Mining Project: A project involving mining activities, either open-cast or underground, or both.

These terms provide a foundational understanding of the terminology used in the context of groundwater management practices.
